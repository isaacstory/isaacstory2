## Lab Commentary & Recommendations

### Beneficial Flora
One or more of the expected or beneficial bacteria are low in this specimen. Bifidobacterium and Lactobacillus are at 1+ levels (healthy levels are 2+ to 4+). The beneficial flora have many health-protecting effects in the gut including digestion of proteins and carbohydrates, manufacture of vitamins, and prevention of pathogen proliferation.

### Dysbiotic Flora
- **Enterobacter cloacae complex**: Gram-negative bacterium considered dysbiotic at levels of 3+ or greater. It is an opportunistic pathogen associated with diarrhea in children. E. cloacae complex possesses inducible ß-lactamases; avoid ß-lactam-inhibitor drugs.
- **Klebsiella spp**: Considered dysbiotic at 3-4+ levels. Has potential to cause intestinal, lung, urinary tract, and wound infections. Has been linked to ankylosing spondylitis and myasthenia gravis.

### Lysozyme Elevation
Lysozyme is elevated (762 ng/mL vs ≤500 reference). This is a biomarker of inflammatory immune response in the gut. Moderate elevations are commonly associated with significant overgrowth of enteropathogens such as yeast, dysbiotic or pathogenic bacteria. May be remediated with elimination of offending microorganisms and use of anti-inflammatory nutraceuticals.

### Treatment Considerations
- Supplementation with probiotics or consumption of fermented foods (yogurt, kefir, miso, tempeh)
- Soluble fiber and polyphenols may help increase beneficial bacteria
- For Enterobacter cloacae: Ceftazidime, Ciprofloxacin, or Sulfamethoxazole/Trimethoprim are susceptible options
- For Klebsiella: Most tested antibiotics show susceptibility except Ampicillin
- Natural agents with high sensitivity: Grapefruit Seed Extract, Silver, Oregano, Uva Ursi
- Anti-inflammatory nutraceuticals: quercetin, vitamin C, curcumin, gamma-linoleic acid, omega-3 fatty acids, aloe vera
- GI mucosa support: zinc, beta-carotene, pantothenic acid, L-glutamine
